The Framework

Three Steps. Every Conversation. Every Time.

The ALS Framework™ is not a script — it's a way of thinking about every sales conversation that puts the customer's needs at the center.

A

Ask

The first step isn't about you or your product. It's about them. Asking the right questions at the start of a conversation is what separates a trusted advisor from a salesperson who just shows up and throws numbers.

Most salespeople rush to the presentation because they're nervous about silence. The ALS Framework trains your team to be comfortable asking — and comfortable waiting for the real answer.

  • Ask open-ended questions that invite the customer to share
  • Uncover the real need behind the stated need
  • Learn their timeline, budget signals, and decision process
  • Build rapport before you ever mention a product
  • Let them tell you how to sell to them
In Practice — Shed Sales Example

"Tell me a little about what you're planning to use this for — is this mainly for storage, a workshop, something else?"

"We need storage but my husband also wants a workspace for his tools. We've been trying to share the garage and it's not working."

"That makes sense — so you really need two things in one building. How much time does he typically spend out there? Is this a weekend hobby or more regular?"

Notice: no product has been mentioned yet. That's intentional.

L

Listen

Asking good questions only works if you actually hear the answers. Listening in sales means more than staying quiet — it means processing what the customer is really telling you, catching the emotion behind the words, and noticing what they're not saying.

This is the step most salespeople skip because they're focused on their next move. Train your team to slow down here, and watch their close rate climb.

  • Don't interrupt — let the customer finish their thought
  • Take notes — it shows respect and improves retention
  • Reflect back what you heard before moving forward
  • Pay attention to hesitation, emotion, and priorities
  • Ask follow-up questions based on what they just said
In Practice — Post Frame Builder Example

"We've been getting quotes for a while but honestly we haven't pulled the trigger because we just don't know who to trust. Last company never called us back."

"That's really frustrating — especially on a project this size. So follow-through and communication are really important to you through this process?"

"Exactly. We just want someone who actually does what they say they're going to do."

The rep just discovered the real objection — not price, not timeline. Now they can address it directly.

S

Solve

Now — and only now — do you present your solution. But because you asked and listened first, this isn't a generic pitch. It's a targeted response to exactly what this specific customer told you they need.

When you solve in the context of their situation, it doesn't feel like a sale. It feels like a recommendation from someone who genuinely understood them. That's when closing becomes easy.

  • Present your solution in terms of their specific needs
  • Reference exactly what they told you earlier
  • Address the hesitations you heard before they bring them up
  • Offer options — not one-size-fits-all
  • Make the next step feel natural, not pressured
In Practice — Barndominium Builder Example

"Based on what you've shared — the workshop space for daily use, the storage side for the equipment, and the fact that you want this done before fall — here's what I'd recommend and why it fits exactly what you described..."

"And you mentioned follow-through was important to you — I want you to know here's exactly what happens from the day you sign to the day we're done. No surprises."

The close isn't a technique. It's the natural result of everything that came before it.
